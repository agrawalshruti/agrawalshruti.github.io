AMMA

Asthma Management and Monitoring Application

client: designlab + google.org

my role: user experience, interaction design

Background

My great uncle has suffered from asthma for most of his life. Today, three decades after first being diagnosed with asthma, his lungs have atrophied by 15%. Because of asthma he was prevented from doing nearly any physical activity for years and has developed other diseases like adult onset diabetes overtime and chronic fatigue. Corticosteriods help him manage his chronic illness, but they sometimes have side-effects that can’t be reversed. Our family constantly worries he can have an asthma attack at any time and not know what caused it.

There are 25 million Americans, including my uncle, affected by asthma today. Asthma is responsible for 14.2 million physician visits and 1.8 million ER visits each year and growth in global air pollution means that these numbers with only increase over time. Costs to treat and manage asthma this chronic asthma are rising.

In order to help these asthma sufferers, Google.org has created a groundbreaking wearable technology, which measures its wearer’s breathing patterns, blood pressure, steps and sleeping patterns and other vital statistics to prevent future attacks. The AMMA band even signals nearby hospitals and doctors when it detects symptoms requiring emergency attention.

Overview

In order to make this treatment a more multipoint solution, Google.org has paired up with DesignLab to create an Asthma Monitoring and Management Application (AMMA). AMMA’s mobile companion app organized adaptive medical data and helped facilitate customized action plans, so asthma patients can self-manage their illness and prevent future attacks. As AMMA’s experience designer, my role was to build a user-centric, user-friendly application that made keeping asthma under control as easy as possible for its users.

Stage 1

The Process

Central questions

I began designing for AMMA’s mobile companion app by asking a few key questions —

Who were AMMA’s primary users?

What were they trying to accomplish with the app and what were their expectations?

Was there previous research to support these findings?

What were the most important functions of the app to the user?

Who were the major competitors in the field and what are they doing effectively/ineffectively to help their users and how?

Exploring User Types

To answer my questions, I researched user stories and two major asthma studies, which revealed three key points —

  • (one) asthma affected a diverse demographic; it is complex and growing global challenge
  • (two) the majority of sufferers supported the development of technologies that would help them self-manage their asthma, including action plans
  • (three) 85% of people wanted to share their medical information and communicate with their doctors in full online

This early stage research revealed that the AMMA app had a lot of potential. It showed patient-doctor communication was integral to the app, including mutually agreed upon action plans, — and that individualization of the care was also very important.

I used this research to guide me while I constructed provisional personas.

User Personas

User Journey Mapping

Personas provided an introduction to the kind of problems asthma sufferers might face. They gave a name and face to empathize with, but still lacked a more chronological overview of AMMA’s users; so I drafted something that was more of a timeline — a user journey map.

With the user journey map, it became easier to see how the mini stories about different users compared. The high-level view of user behaviors before and after app use also led to more insightful questions about user behavior. I had to think about the user environment more deeply.

In Essence

  • the user journey addressed the users' asthma frustrations outside the app
  • gave more context to user experiences
  • and it meant asking a more holistic line of questioning

Interviews

Q & A

Preliminary research eventually resulted in more informed user interview questions. Before asking any questions, though, I used the following guidelines to make sure not to lead users - in efforts to prevent data manipulation. During my interviews with 12 sufferers and 3 doctors, I reminded myself the interview questions were meant to

  • Help users fulfill their needs
  • Empathize with users to identify user gain & pain points
  • Enlighten previously held beliefs/assumptions about users + app
  • Translate which features/functions are most important users to create a feature hierarchy
  • Build relationships among user experiences

Stage 2

Feature Mapping

I organized the interview results into two categories - Findings & Deductions. Findings were the literal summaries of interview answers and deductions were how these findings were relevant to app features.

Both deductions and preliminary research confirmed that: asthma self management involved “identifying and avoiding triggers, altering the environment remembering both preventer and reliever medications, recognising worsening symptoms, adjusting treatment and determining when to contact a doctor”. The challenging part now was understanding how these features would work with one another.

App Map

Competitive Analysis

AMMA’s app map was challenging to build. With features that were so interconnected, streamlining them into separate categories wasn’t easy. Even after finishing it, I still felt a little unresolved about the map. I conducted competitive analysis and compared user interfaces and user testimonials about app features to see what others have done.

AMMA’s main competitors confirmed that focusing on feature efficacy was more important than quantity.

Feature Matrix

Stage 3

Wireframing and Sketching

I knew some of my suspicions about the app features wouldn’t become clear until I started sketching. Because my users expressed a desire to quickly enter and exit the app, I tried to consolidate feature availability to a central dashboard.

From that central dashboard users could view the weather, notifications, adaptive statistics synced with the AMMA band and access their action plan. All these components would cumulatively decide if the user’s asthma was under control or not.

Visualizing the user flow

I drafted another round of sketches and played around with the screen sequence a little more loosely. But it wasn't until I used feature map that I could visualize what the app more clearly.

Low-fidelity Wireframes

Prioritizing

Prioritizing features expedited the design process. I used early stage research, including user stories and interviews to mockup low-fidelity wireframes. These features included action plans, weather notifications, users symptom logs, mood assessment and adaptive statistics. The AMMA band would also be part of the main dashboard and sync its data to show users updated stats on the home screen.

Prototype

Using the feature map as a guide, I created a prototype to test the usability of a few major user flows: the action plan, notifications & settings.

To incentivize users, I introduced a “peak flow challenge” and also rewarded users with badges if they logged symptoms and and were responsible about taking their medications.

In the notifications user flow I included triggers, doctor messages, and asthma tips as three separate tabs on the same screen. I wondered whether a more general notifications screen would be more accessible.

I decided to wait and see how users responded during testing to clear my doubts.

Stage 5

User Testing and Final Iterations

User testing confirmed that many users found the status update process to be a bit unclear. They felt that the connection between updating your status and action plans was ambiguous. With regards to notifications, users didn’t find the user flow difficult to navigate, but thought different types of notifications could a little more obvious and easier to access from the main dashboard.

Most users did appreciate the overall visual design, weather and pollen count notifications, alert microcards and vital statistics readings. Some said that settings sidebar could be better organized to include action plan user flow screens and there were also a few UI issues that could be easily amended. Users were encouraged to use the app more with the badge, but users said the general app design was also an incentive to user the app.

Overall, future changes would likely involve

  • Simplifying the action plan user flow
  • Making user flow screens more findable
  • Creating a more encompassing app notification system
  • Giving users more context for each screen and/or feature
  • Creating a ‘data hub,’ where users can access infographs more easily
  • The option to forward info to the users’ doctor or even chat with him or her would be integrated in more parts of the application

Because AMMA was such an ambitious project, multiple iterations were expected and are likely in the future. Arguably, the largest takeaway from AMMA was learning how to integrate complex app features as seamlessly as possible.

Fulfilling both a large range of sufferers and also doctors needs was also incredible challenging. As my interviews revealed, all sufferers had different degrees of asthma and translating these results into the app was not easy. Thinking of how to work more precisely with the AMMA band remains part of the plan.

In the end, I learned a great deal from this project and have intriguing ideas for its future.
