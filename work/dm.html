Dignity Meals

Safe and Healthy Food Redistribution

client: designlab + dignity meals

my role: user experience, interaction design

Understanding the problem

Every year 40% of food produced in the United States goes uneaten, 1 in 4 calories is never consumed and $165 billion is spent on essentially producing waste. The average American consumer discards nearly 10x as much food as the average Southeast Asian consumer.

All this food waste comes as a shock when 15-25 million Americans go hungry every day and 1 in every 6 Americans is food insecure. Reducing food waste by just 15% could prevent nationwide hunger, while educating consumers and retailers on ways to effectively redistribute and reuse food could make all the difference.

Solution

Dignity Meals is a start-up whose goal is to help solve the food waste crisis in Chicago.

For them it was most important that the app

  • provided easy access to donors
  • addressed donor guidelines, so donations were safe and hygienic
  • appeared credible and reliant to its users

Breakthrough

My research really came to a turning point after an in-depth interview with a large donor agency, City Harvest, that only donates to smaller donor agencies. After speaking with City Harvest’s donations coordinator, who previously worked for the Coalition for the Homeless for five years, I knew an app designed to provide access to wholesale and small scale donations had potential.

One way to approach this problem was designing the app using a point system, much like in the case of pantries. Smaller agencies and individuals would get a set number of free points to claim donations, whereas farms and agricultural entities would need to purchase points for large scale donations. Another way was simply to make all donations free and have larger service organizations pay the app a service fee.

The app could also have a volunteer feature, where the app would provide an upgraded feature, so larger donors and recipients could organize donations and discounted sales inside the app. Whereas the standard version of the app would allow recipients to pick up at a local Dignity Meals pickup location.

Stage 1

The Process

Central questions

My first step in designing for the user involved visualizing what different user types needed to accomplish from the app. I started by asking —

Who are my donors? recipients? volunteer agencies?

What challenges keep them from rescuing food?

How could users be educated within the app about food donation?

What kind of expectations would the user have of an app like Dignity Meals?

How could these expectations be translated into product requirements?

How would the user types interact inside the app?

Exploring User Types

To begin answering, I crafted three personas: Mike, Becky & Melinda.

Someone like Mike, for instance, could post leftovers from his restaurant that day or tons of overstock due to a misorder for a discounted rate. Whereas Melinda needed to feed the women of her shelter and had funding to purchase Mike’s discounted surplus pasta. Becky, on the other hand, could benefit from Mike’s leftover lasagna from that day — cooked and untouched.

User Flow Mapping

  • Mike, as a donor, needed to create listings
  • Recipients Becky and Melinda needed to claim donation listings
  • and Melinda, as a service organization leader of many shelters, needed to organize donations, possibly a "volunteer upgrade feature"

Interviews

Q & A

User flow mapping effectively segued to user interviews.

I wanted to know if

  • Dignity Meals would be useful? how? and to whom?
  • Dignity Meals could be usable to each usertype? and how?
  • How did user needs differ?
  • How features of the app could be prioritized?
  • these features could be streamlined (to avoid feature creep)?

Detailed Results

Donors

  • Most sources are open to giving donations, but are afraid of liability & regulations
  • Lack of time and resources prevents them from donating
  • The leftovers they do have are edible, but may be deemed “unappetizing” by high restaurant standards
  • Still some leftovers can be donated and the rest can be or are given to animals i.e. farms
  • Large scale supermarket/donors deliver food directly to a city central foodbank
  • Large donors will not donate to individuals because it's not worth it

RECIPIENT (Small donation agencies that serve individuals)

  • Usually get food from other social and poverty organizations that take care of the administration and deal with donors
  • Could use funding spent on food for other purposes e.g organize activities for children, healthcare costs
  • Need more helping hands, resources (extra coordinators)

VOLUNTEER / NONPROFIT (Large donation agency serves smaller agencies )

  • Need to support regular donors (weekly)
  • Educate donors and recipients using a donor kit and especially about tax incentives
  • Get funding from the government / private donors
  • Have a minimum requirement of 50lbs for food rescue - otherwise, if it meets all other regulations, gets redirected to donor agencies
  • Find that awareness is growing with what donors can donate and that awareness really helps speed up the whole process
  • Because the need is so great accepting and allocating donations on an individual basis is too overwhelming to manage for them

Stage 2

Feature Mapping

The interview findings revealed that avoiding feature creep would be complicated. Using an app map stressed the connections among features, making it easier to streamline them.

Part of this streamlining was enabling multiple user dashboards post-login. Before, I intended to divide user-types pre-login, but since all users needed to view donation listings, it was more user-friendly to enable donor, recipient and volunteer dashboards post-login.

Stage 3

Wireframing and Sketching

Though the app map helped in working through a few hurdles, I still struggled with picturing what the home screen would look like, so I used sketches as a way to loosely draw out main app screens.

It helped to have sketches and user flows next to each other.

Since my interviewees stressed the turnover rate of donations, I wanted the posts themselves to be agile — swift, convenient, easily scannable.

Having one post taking up a whole screen was too weighty and thumbnail size posts were too tiny for the content that was needed in a food rescue app.

Eventually, I made the posts into microcards. Designing the posts as cards made them appear dynamic; swipeable, stackable, pullable and pushable. The association was fitting for the app and from a technical standpoint, made for powerful animations.

Prototype

I chose to focus to focus on the donor/recipient behavior and the posting dashboard first. I used the wireframes to build a gray scale prototype and tested it on my first testers. I told them to complete three actions — add a donation, claim a donation and find where these bookmarked items were kept after bookmarking them.

Just by observing their behavior, I gleaned that there was clear user conflict between the triple dot menu and its relationship to the action button. The testers couldn’t tell whether they needed to go to the triple dot menu or action button to add a listing.

Similarly, the claiming donation user flow also had one glaring issue. Testers didn’t make the leap from the more details button to making a claim.

Stage 4

UI Design

Moodboard · styletile · logo

Option 1 - Textured, rounded, solid

This moodboard takes pattern-like elements from printmaking and illustration. Visual design elements are meant to be approachable, trustworthy and safe, much like the Dignity Meals brand.

Option 2 - Fluid, lucid, playful

This moodboard is inspired by the fluid contours of fruits and vegetables as well as, watercolor. Different levels of opacity and, colors and forms seem diverse, friendly, and safe, like the Dignity Meals philosophy.

Stage 5

User Testing and Final Iterations

Results

Mainly, I learned that

  • some users still had a lot of trouble with the action button / triple dot menu i.e. figuring out how to initiate a donation
  • felt the pickup checklist felt kind of out of place
  • all users appreciated swiping as a way to browse food categories and wanted to see swiping in other parts of the app
  • the point system needed more of an explanation and better integration
  • font size was too small & links blended in
  • bookmarks could be clearer
