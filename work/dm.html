Dignity Meals

Safe and Healthy Food Redistribution

client: designlab + dignity meals

my role: user experience, interaction design

Understanding the problem

Every year 40% of food produced in the United States goes uneaten, 1 in 4 calories is never consumed and $165 billion is spent on essentially producing waste. 25% of food bought by consumers is directly trashed. For reasons including lack of awareness, confusion over sell by dates and poor storage conditions, the average American consumer discards nearly 10x as much food as the average Southeast Asian consumer.

All this food waste comes as a shock when 15-25 million Americans go hungry every day and 1 in every 6 Americans is food insecure. Reducing food waste by just 15% could prevent nationwide hunger, while educating consumers and retailers on ways to effectively redistribute and reuse food can make all the difference.

Solution

Dignity Meals is a start up whose goal is to help solve the food waste crisis and eventually aid world hunger — starting with the city of Chicago. The Dignity Meals engineering team asked for a designer to conduct market research and implement design that would serve their users best. It became my role as a product designer to understand who their users were, what the users needed from the app, and make sure both business and user goals were met.

For them it was most important that the app

  • provide easy access to donors
  • addressed donor guidelines, so donations were safe and hygienic
  • appeared credible and reliant to its users

After conducting interviews and market research, though, I thought designing an app that served multiple user types — individual donors, donor agencies and farming and agriculture entities could be an effective possibility. Like the Dignity Meals team, my research showed that thinking in terms of solely individual recipients wouldn’t work.

I found those in need tended to lack the resources, like a phone, to gain access to donations. At the same time, donors with larger donations didn’t have the resources to donate individuals and even needed the means to donate to service organizations.

Breakthrough

My research really came to a turning point after an in-depth interview with a large donor agency, City Harvest, that only donates to smaller donor agencies. After speaking with City Harvest’s donations coordinator, who previously worked for the Coalition for the Homeless for five years, I knew an app designed to provide access to wholesale and small scale donations had potential.

One way to approach this problem was designing the app using a point system, much like in the case of pantries. Smaller agencies and individuals would get a set number of free points to claim donations, whereas farms and agricultural entities would need to purchase points for large scale donations. Or another way is simply make all donations free and have larger service organizations pay the app a service fee.

The app could also have a volunteer feature, where the app could provide an upgraded feature, so larger donors and recipients can organize donations and discounted sales inside the app. Whereas the standard version of the app would allow recipients to pick up at a local Dignity Meals pickup location.

Though these ideas were ambitious, I wanted the app to solve as many of its users concerns as possible, so they could seamlessly donate and rescue food.

Stage 1

The Process

Central questions

My first step in designing for the user involved visualizing what different user types needed to accomplish from the app. I started by asking —

What kind of background would a donor have? a recipient? a volunteer agency?

What challenges keep them from rescuing food?

How could users be educated within the app about food donation?

What kind of expectations would the user have of an app like Dignity Meals?

How could these expectations be translated into product requirements?

How do the user types interact inside the app?

Exploring User Types

To answer these concerns I crafted three provisional personas — Mike, Becky & Melina.

I was interested in getting to know how an app like Dignity Meals could fit into their daily activities, so I included their day-to-day routine, alongside their larger worries and frustrations.

Through personas, it became easier see how all user types could live in same app. I started to think of Dignity Meals like a center of exchange.

Someone like Mike could post leftovers from his restaurant that day or tons of overstock due to a misorder for a discounted rate. Whereas Melinda needed to feed the women of her shelter and had funding to purchase Mike’s discounted surplus pasta. Becky, on the other hand, might benefit from Mike’s leftover lasagna from that day — cooked and untouched.

These personas were, of course, speculative and needed to be further qualified via interviews and surveys with real-life user interviews. But, as a stepping stone, they were pretty useful.

User Flow Mapping

I paired Mike, Becky, and Melinda, each with one corresponding user flow. Though there are many actions other than these in the app. I chose one main user flow for each user type. Inspired by a few successful donation apps Copia, Food Cowboy, Zero Percent and TangoTab as well as general consignment and resale marketplaces, eBay, Poshmark, Mercari and Depop, I designed an app with a layout that involved creating a listing/post.

  • Mike, as a donor, needed to create listings
  • Both Becky and Melinda would need to be able to claim donation listings
  • Melinda, as a service organization leader to many shelters, could opt for an app upgrade feature that provided volunteers with a delivery schedule, organizes donations and routes the user to donation sites and shelters

User flow mapping effectively segued to user interviews. User flows made it easier to understand the kind of questions I need to ask my interviewees.

Interviews

Q & A

I was able to get in touch with 9 potential donors, mostly restaurants, cafes, bakeries and 1 chain supermarket, 1 large service organization that only deals with large scale donation and other agencies and a 1 small donation agency, a day care, reaching individual donors.

I wanted to know if

  • Dignity Meals would be useful? how? and to whom?
  • Dignity Meals could be usable to each usertype? and how?
  • How did user needs differ?
  • How features of the app could be prioritized?
  • these features be streamlined (to avoid feature creep)?

Results

Donors

  • Most sources are open to giving donations, but are afraid of liability & regulations
  • Lack of time and resources prevents them from donating
  • The leftovers they do have are edible, but may be deemed “unappetizing” by high restaurant standards
  • Still some leftovers can be donated and the rest can be or are given to animals i.e. farms
  • Large scale supermarket/donors deliver food directly to a city central foodbank
  • Large donors will not donate to individuals because its not worth it

RECIPIENT (Small donation agencies that serves individuals)

  • Usually get food from other social and poverty organizations that take care of the administration and deal with the donors
  • Could use the money funding spent on food to other resources e.g organize activities for children, healthcare costs
  • Need more helping hands, resources (extra coordinators)

VOLUNTEER / NONPROFIT (Large donation agency serves smaller agencies )

  • Need to support regular donors (weekly)
  • Educate donors and recipients using a donor kit and especially about tax incentives
  • Get funding from the government / private donors
  • Have a minimum requirement of 50lbs for food rescue - otherwise, if it meets all other regulations, gets redirected to donor agencies
  • Find that an awareness is growing with what donors can donate and that awareness really helps speed up the whole process
  • Because the need is so great accepting and allocating donations on an individual basis is too overwhelming to manage for them

Stage 2

Feature Mapping

The interview findings revealed that avoiding feature creep would be complicated. Using an app map stressed the connections among features, making it easier to streamline them.

Part of this streamlining was enabling multiple user dashboards post-login. Before, I intended to divide user-types pre-login, but since all users needed to view donation listings, it was more user friendly to enable donor, recipient and volunteer dashboards post-login.

App Map

UX Strategy Blue

Though the app map helped in working through a few hurdles, I still struggled with picturing what the home screen would look like. I referred back to the UX Strategy Blue for help to think from the user’s perspective, keep it as simple as possible.

I tried to work through how the three key actions - adding a listing, claiming a donation and using donor points - could be incorporated onto the homescreen.

Stage 3

Wireframing and Sketching

It became too overwhelming to go from app map directly to digital wireframes, so I used sketches as a way to loosely draw out main app screens.

It helped to have sketches and user flows next to each other.

I kept sketching and developed more detailed screens for donor/recipient and volunteer users. I knew I wanted the posts themselves to be agile — swift, convenient, easily scannable. Since I was dealing with food and my interviewees stressed the turnover rate of donations, I knew that this aspect should be part of the interface design.

The current model of having one post taking up a whole screen was too weighty. At the same time, thumbnail size posts were too tiny and read more like a social networking app than a food rescue one.

Eventually, I made the posts into microcards. Designing the posts as cards made them appear dynamic; swipeable, stackable, pullable and pushable. The association was fitting for the app and from a technical standpoint, made for interesting animations.

Prioritizing

From detailed sketches, I quickly moved on to digital wireframes. I annotated the wireframes to show which parts of the user interface were relevant to each respective user-type.

Since I was still having difficulty placing the advance volunteer workflow inside the app,I chose to focus more on the donor - recipient user behavior and the general postings dashboard first.

Prototype

I used the wireframes to build a gray scale prototype and tested it on my first two testers. I told them to complete three actions — add a donation, claim a donation and find where these bookmarked items were kept after bookmarking them.

Just by observing their behavior, I gleaned that there was clear user conflict between the triple dot menu and its relationship to action button. The two testers couldn’t tell whether they needed to go to the triple dot menu or action button to add a listing. Since I wasn’t sure if this was a adoptive behavior conflict, I waited to see how my other testers behaved.

Similarly, the claiming donation user flow also had one glaring issues. Testers didn’t make the leap between the more details button to making a claim.

Stage 4

UI Design

Moodboard · styletile · logo

Option 1 - Textured, rounded, solid

This moodboard takes pattern-like elements from printmaking and illustration. Visual design elements are meant to be approachable, trustworthy and safe, much like the Dignity Meals brand.

Option 2 - Fluid, lucid, playful

This moodboard is inspired by the fluid contours of fruits and vegetables as well as, watercolor. Different levels of opacity and, colors and forms seem diverse, friendly, and safe, like the Dignity Meals philosophy.

Stage 5

User Testing and Final Iterations

I ultimately ended up testing 8 users. Most users were students who fell in the donor / recipient category and a few working professionals with nonprofit / service backgrounds.

I used an affinity map to group their thoughts/actions. Because they tested the same color prototypes, I only documented 6 of the users in the affinity map.

I first wrote down their thoughts / actions on post-its and scrap notebooks, then grouped the, attributed titles and digitized them.

This whole process was insightful and cathartic all at the same time. It was a relief to know how the users felt about the prototype and this crucial information became essential to devising new solutions.

Results

Mainly, I learned that

  • Some users still had a lot of trouble with the action button / triple dot menu i.e. figuring out how to initiate a donation
  • some users had no trouble with it at all
  • All users found the claim process to be jumpy and needing more context
  • even felt the pickup checklist felt kind of out of place
  • all users appreciated swiping as a way to browse food categories and wanted to see swiping in other parts of the app
  • They thought the point system needed more of an explanation and more integrated
  • The font was too small in some places and the links blended in
  • Both bookmark and bookmark dashboard could be clearer and cleaner

Because of these user testing results, I made some preliminary design changes, but knew that the larger changes would involve further study. I updated the UI font sizes, links, and added contrast to ambiguous parts of the screen. Soon after, I sketched out 6 alternate solutions to address user friction during the add a donation process.
