Instashop

Fresh and easy grocery delivery to your doorstep

client: instashop + designlab

my role: user experience, user interface

Background

Goodmarket is a grocery franchise originating in the United States. To increase revenue and overall brand appeal, Goodmarket had decided that they needed to release a pilot version of an online grocery ordering program — Instashop. As Instashop’s product designer, my role became to quickly implement user-friendly digital designs that met both user and business goals.

Business Goals

expand customer base & market share

  • Focus on busy young professionals
  • Attract an additional user demographic (mostly younger)

design and release a pilot program in ny & sf

  • Develop a seamless and efficient grocery app
  • Begin with 100 products first
  • Expand pilot program to other cities eventually

improve customer experience & convenience

  • Generate more revenue & value for the whole company

User Goals

navigate app easily and efficiently

  • Seamless checkout
  • Secure payment and easy re-ordering
  • Customized product recommendations

quick delivery and quality products

  • Add from a large selection of items
  • Same day delivery

have a positive user experience

  • Groceries arrive fresh and clean
  • Overall experience is easier than in person

Challenge

The challenge in this project was in finding a way to structure and organize Instashop’s 100+ product inventory.

For the design to accomplish its other set goals, the user needed to be able to navigate the site successfully first. A confused and aggravated user that couldn’t locate a product would leave the app at once without purchasing anything.

Stage 1

The Process

Exploring User Behavior

The checkout user flow had showed how browsing, searching and locating items were all principal app functions that relied heavily on how product categories were named.

It visually confirmed that at the heart of a successful navigation was constructing a user-centric taxonomy for Instashop’s product categories.

Stage 2

Information Architecture

But it was card sorting, in the end, that proved to be the most helpful research tool in determining how to organize the app.

I didn’t want to lead the users in a certain direction, so I didn’t provide them with any categories and told them come up with their own.

One mistake I did make, at the time, was telling users to create as few categories as possible. Having few categories could have made it harder to find similarities between them later on, and second and third tier categories amongst users. It got rid of some data that might have helped in the future.

Given the chance to conduct the tests again, I’d split them into two tests (one) that instructs users to make general categories and (two) to make as few categories as possible.

Card Sorting

Before I had checked the results of the test, I separated the products into my own categories. I figured the card sorting test would have more or less similar results. Instead, amongst my 6 users, 4 new categories in addition to mine emerged and dozens of product subset variations.

Sitemap

With the card sorting data, I created a sitemap and kept the most popular categories to develop Instashop’s information architecture.

Having IA principles helped streamline app features, especially post wireframing and prototyping.
