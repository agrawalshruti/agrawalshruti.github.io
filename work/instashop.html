Instashop

Fresh and easy grocery delivery to your doorstep

client: instashop + designlab

my role: user experience, user interface

Background

Goodmarket is a grocery franchise originating in the United States. Even though their customer satisfaction ratings had remained relatively consistent over the last four years, recently their market shares had been decreasing by 8% each year.

Goodmarket researchers had determined that this decline was mostly due to the global increase in online grocery ordering and delivery products. Because it was quicker, more convenient, and provided equally fresh food - customers preferred online shopping to that in store.

To increase revenue and overall brand appeal, Goodmarket had decided that they needed to release a pilot version of an online grocery ordering program — Instashop. They would launch Instashop’s mobile app and website in New York and San Francisco in a few months. As Instashop’s product designer, my role became to quickly implement user-friendly digital designs that met both user and business goals.

Challenge

In general, the user and business goals were pretty straight-forward. The challenge in this project was in finding a way to structure and organize Instashop’s 100+ product inventory.

In order for the app to work seamlessly and efficiently and generate revenue, users needed to be able to find and purchase the item they wanted out of these 100+ products easily. A confused and aggravated user that couldn’t locate a product would leave the app at once without purchasing anything.

For the design to accomplish its other set goals, the user needed to be able to navigate the site successfully first. At the heart of this successful navigation was constructing a user-centric taxonomy for Instashop’s product categories.

Stage 1

The Process

Exploring User Behavior

I tried using empathy maps and personas to better understand user behavior. When that didn’t prove as helpful as I thought, I used a user flow.

The checkout user flow had showed how browsing, searching and locating items were all principal app functions that relied heavily on how product categories were named.

It visually confirmed that without a well-founded taxonomy, users couldn’t even add to cart.

Stage 2

Information Architecture

But it was card sorting, in the end, that proved to be the most helpful research tool in determining how to organize the app.

I printed out the product names, cut them into individual paper strips and had the users group them into their own categories. I didn’t want to lead the users in a certain direction, so I didn’t provide them with any categories and told them come up with their own.

One mistake I did make, at the time, was telling users to create as few categories as possible. Having few categories could have made it harder to find similarities between them later on, and second and third tier categories amongst users. It got rid of some data that might have helped in the future.

Given the chance to conduct the tests again, I’d split them into two tests (one) that instructs users to make general categories and (two) to make as few categories as possible.

Card Sorting

Before I had checked the results of the test, I separated the products into my own categories. I figured the card sorting test would have more or less similar results.

Instead, amongst my 6 users, 4 new categories in addition to mine emerged and dozens of product subset variations. There were some categories I hadn’t even imagined; clever ones that really helped and obscure ones that I saved for later.

Sitemap

All the card sorting data, whether it contradicted or supported my prediction, was insightful. With it, I created a chart and kept the most popular categories to form Instashop’s information architecture.

I took the results to build a sitemap, and although there was room for improvement and further testing, because of time constraints, I started building wireframes.

I created a small diagram summarizing Information Architecture principles to guide me while I designed the mobile and web user experience. Having these IA principles on hand helped streamline app features.

I had to ask myself if the feature design was clear, usable, learnable and credible. Was it accessible and did it satisfy findability? Were there parts of the design that were surprisingly delightful?

Post wireframing and prototyping, I used the diagram as a checklist to see which parts of the design could use more work.

Stage 3

Wireframes

I chose to start with mobile wireframes

  • (one) because most of my target users were young business professionals that were constantly on the move and
  • (two) because it was easier prioritize content going from a smaller to a larger screen

Desktop wireframes soon followed. Because they were grounded in research, the product categories for mobile and web stayed the same. However, I struggled a bit with the different visual layouts for the products.

I knew listview wouldn’t work for desktop because it wouldn’t optimize the screen space, but with all the content required for each product it would work nicely on mobile.

I’d later conduct user testing to tell me more about how to handle this challenge.

Stage 5

User Testing and Final Iterations

User Testing Results

Mobile user testing revealed that many of the users had similar difficulties with app navigation.

I used my questions to help name column titles and divided up the results below, but this method of data organization felt too rigid and separated from the user.

So to try an alternate approach, I wrote down the users’ direct thoughts and feelings about the app on post-its and grouped the ones that were most alike.

Much like during the card-sorting exercise, I labelled the categories after I grouped content. This method proved to be more user-centric and was easier to incorporate into the final iterations.
