Prahs

Native Advertising for Prahs Energy Drink

client: T Brand Studio + designlab

my role: design and research

Background

This past spring I attended a class at SVA with Rachel Gogel of T Brand Studio. The larger project for the class was designing the UX of a native advertising piece for Prahs — an energy drink brand.

Concept

Prahs’ brief stated that they wanted their innovative, competitive and bold spirit to be central to the design. They also wanted to incorporate the Olympic games as part of their campaign, but weren’t allowed to mention them directly.

My solution to the brief was two-fold. Prahs would be innovative by promoting solar energy, a technology of the future, while being competitive and bold by comparing solar energy advancements in developed vs. those in developing nations.

I thought Brazil, the location for the 2016 Olympics, and the United States, the country that invested in solar energy most, could initiate a larger discussion about global solar energy spending. This way the content would shed light on a relevant issues - the millions unemployed in Brazil, the global rapid depletion of nonrenewable natural resources and solar energy potential - like the NYTimes brand - but also fulfill the client’s needs.

Execution

Without addressing the Olympics explicitly, Prahs user experience was executed to have a gamified and diplomatic appeal.

As the user scrolled down the site, small flags acting miniature representations of international nations animated on scroll, with two countries, one developed and the other developing, always facing off one another.

For mobile this visual parallel was more obvious than on desktop, but the idea behind this face off was that this animation and competition - engaged site readers and promoted brand awareness.

Stage 1

Discovery

Giving the native piece context

Designing the user experience wasn’t enough. An important learning lesson in the class was seeing the native piece as part of a larger brand campaign and understanding how it fit into a content marketing ecosystem.

A user discovery map helped maked these content relationships clearer. It got me thinking about how the user would “discover” the content and how it would be shared with others. Would the user find the article through

  • Social?
  • The brand main page?
  • A link on a accompanying print piece?

This made clear one thing for sure — getting people to the site was also part of the design process for Prahs

Stage 2

Visual Inspiration

Moodboard · styletile · logo

Option 1 - Techy Ultramodern

Thin, crisp lines with geometric overlays and black backdrop project a strong technological emphasis and spacious aesthetic. Bright contrast gives a gamified, futuristic look.

Option 2 - RetroFuturism

Warm colorful gradience and paper-like texture, with clean geometric shapes highlights both a sunny mood and retrofuturistic ideal.

Option 3 - Photoreal gradience

Smooth black silhouettes against a backdrop with varying shades of sunlight higlights nature’s beauty and the beauty of the human form. Typography is either black, more likely white, and rounded, but crisp to maintain elegant user interface

Stage 3

Wireframing and Sketching

Out of the entire design process, though, the wireframing and sketching stage was probably the most challenging. I used previous TBrand paid posts to help guide the visual design of the Prahs paid piece. To figure out how to work with pacing, I made sure to show screen breakpoints with white lines in the wireframe.

Eventually, trying to make the site responsive while maintaining an integrity to the content was probably where I struggled most. I went through several wireframe iterations and realized my greatest failure was not deciding whether I wanted the content to be long form or more like a listicle for both web and mobile early enough.

Final Reflections

Had I had more time, I would have spent it on brainstorming more ways of breaking down the content visually and prototyping the wireframes earlier on. I might have scraped the long form idea all together and stuck with a series animated quizzes to educated my users. There are several possibilities.

However, I still learned loads from the experience overall, especially about how to think of web and media content as agile as the technologies that support it.
